General plan

Archaeological excavations at Kınık Höyük started in 2011 as a decennial joint project of the University of Pavia and the Institute for the Study of the Ancient World, New York University, with the participation of the Universities of Niğde and Erzurum.

The site of Kınık Höyük is located in Southern Cappadocia, in the province of Niğde, at the foot of Melendiz Dağları. This region played a strategic role from prehistory up to modern times, due to its geographical position on the route connecting the Central Anatolian plateau to Cilicia, through the Taurus passes, and, far beyond, to Syria and Mesopotamia. Despite its importance, so far the province of Niğde has received little attention from scholars, particularly as regards the Bronze and Iron ages. With a view to bridging this gap, a team from the University of Pavia carried out an archaeological survey in the region between 2006 and 2009. Investigations involved an area of around 800 km2 between the southern slopes of Melendiz Dağları and the northern fringes of the Bor-Ereğli plain, and enabled us to record 37 archaeological sites dating from the Chalcolithic to the Middle Age. Among these sites, Kınık Höyük was recognized as the most important for the purposes of the research group, being the largest among the sites with significant ceramic surface collections dating to pre-classical periods.

The site is a ca. 20 m high mound, 180m in diameter, rising on a roughly square terrace, which is 300m wide. However, the abundance of ceramics sherds collected in the fields surrounding the mound and the terrace proves the existence of a larger lower town. Including this, the site as a whole measures 24 ha in total. Judging by the ceramics collected during the intensive survey, the site would appear to have been occupied from the Early Bronze Age to the Middle Age, but, considering number and distribution of ceramic sherds, the major occupation phases of the site most likely correspond to the Late Bronze Age (LBA) and the Iron Age (IA).

This latter dating interestingly matches that of the Neo-Hittite monumental rock reliefs and steles found in the region, all of which are attributed to the rulers of Tuwana. Despite its importance in the region during the 8th century BC, this kingdom is still under-investigated by archaeologists. If the site’s current dimensions correspond to those of the 1st millennium BC, Kınık must have been one of the most prominent centers in the reign of Tuwana. The expected LBA levels, moreover, would correspond to the Hittite occupation of the site, when it was one of the towns in the vicinity of Tuwanuwa (later to become Tuwana).

In 2010, geomagnetic and Ground Penetrating Radar (GPR) surveys were performed at Kınık, and the results they produced were very promising. The anomalies, in fact, point to an elliptic stone structure completely encircling the slope of the mound, thus evidencing an underlying fortification wall. Moreover, other anomalies on the top of the mound account for the presence of buildings in this area.

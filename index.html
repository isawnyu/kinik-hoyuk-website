Introduction

The site of Kınık Höyük lies in the southern fringes of ancient Cappadocia, in the modern province of Niğde. In ancient times Southern Cappadocia represented the main transit area towards the Taurus passes, which connected the Central Anatolian plateau to the Mediterranean sea and the Northern Levant. Given this strategic position, the region was continously occupied and traversed since the Prehistory, becoming especially crucial during the Hittite, Neo-Hittite, Persian, Hellenistic, Roman and Medieval periods. During the Middle Iron Age (9th-8th BCE), believed to be the height of the settlement’s activity, Kınık Höyük was located at the heart of the kingdom of Tuwana, a poorly known Neo-Hittite polity documented so far by monumental inscriptions in Southern Cappadocia and some references in contemporary Assyrian records. The particular position of Kınık makes it also a potentially attractive stage in modern touristic itineraries. Close to the North, in fact, are found the traditional destinations of Cappadocia, e.g. Göreme, Avanos, Üçhisar and the Underground Cities. Despite such a high potential from both a historical and cultural point of view, Southern Cappadocia was still an underexplored region until the 2011, when a joint American, Italian and Turkish excavation project started to disclose the remains of one of its most promising sites: Kınık Höyük.
